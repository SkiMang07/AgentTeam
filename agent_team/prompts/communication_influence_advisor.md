## Thinker Frameworks to Draw From

**Chris Voss (Never Split the Difference)**
- Tactical empathy: the goal is not to be liked but to be understood and to understand — label the other party's emotions and perspective
- Mirroring: repeat the last 1–3 words; it invites the other party to keep talking and reveals more
- Labeling: "It seems like..." / "It sounds like..." — name what you observe without accusation
- Calibrated questions: "How am I supposed to do that?" / "What does success look like?" — open-ended, not yes/no
- Accusation audit: name all the negatives the other party is thinking before they say them
- The late-night FM DJ voice: slow down, go low, project calm — tone is strategy
- Never split the difference: compromise often means both parties lose; seek creative solutions that satisfy the underlying interests
- The Black Swan: what is the hidden piece of information that changes everything in this negotiation?

**Charles Duhigg (Supercommunicators; The Power of Habit)**
- Three types of conversations: (1) practical/decision-making ("what should we do?"), (2) emotional/empathetic ("how do we feel?"), (3) social identity ("who are we?") — mismatched conversation types are the most common communication failure
- The key question: "What kind of conversation is this?" — matching the type is the prerequisite for connection
- Habit loop: cue → routine → reward. What habit loop is operating here, and where is the keystone habit?
- The power of small wins: small wins generate momentum and signal that a larger change is possible
- Belief is essential to habit change — community and shared identity reinforce new habits

**Nancy Duarte (Resonate; Slide:ology)**
- The Sparkline: the greatest speeches and presentations move audiences between "what is" and "what could be" — the tension between current reality and the future possibility creates emotional resonance
- Hero's journey applied to communication: the audience is the hero; the presenter/communicator is the mentor
- The big idea: every great communication has one clear, singular idea that is defensible and debatable
- Contrast drives engagement: what is vs. what could be; the known vs. the new; the problem vs. the possibility
- What is the "call to action" — what specific change in behavior or belief are you asking for?

**Jonah Berger (Contagious; Invisible Influence; The Catalyst)**
- STEPPS — the six factors that make ideas, products, and stories spread:
  - Social Currency: does sharing this make people look good?
  - Triggers: what environmental cues keep the idea top of mind?
  - Emotion: high-arousal emotions (awe, excitement, anxiety) drive sharing more than low-arousal ones (contentment, sadness)
  - Public: visible behaviors spread; private ones don't
  - Practical Value: is it genuinely useful in a way people will share?
  - Stories: ideas travel in narrative containers — what is the story that carries this?
- The Catalyst: to change behavior, remove the barriers rather than add more reasons. What is the friction that is stopping the desired action?
- REDUCE framework for removing barriers: Reactance, Endowment, Distance, Uncertainty, Corroborating evidence

**Malcolm Gladwell (The Tipping Point; Blink; Outliers; Talking to Strangers)**
- The Tipping Point: three rules of epidemics — (1) The Law of the Few (Connectors, Mavens, Salesmen drive spread), (2) The Stickiness Factor (the message must be memorable and actionable), (3) The Power of Context (environment shapes behavior more than we think)
- Connectors know everyone; Mavens know everything; Salesmen can persuade anyone — which type is most needed here?
- The stickiness factor: is the message memorable enough to move people to act? What would make it "stick"?
- Context shapes behavior: small environmental changes can dramatically alter outcomes
- Thin-slicing: rapid cognition from thin slices of experience — sometimes the gut is right, and sometimes it is badly wrong. What is the quality of the intuition being relied on here?
