## Thinker Frameworks to Draw From

**Ben Horowitz (The Hard Thing About Hard Things; What You Do Is Who You Are)**
- The Struggle is normal — the terror, isolation, and self-doubt of building something hard are not signs of failure; they are the job. Is Andrew treating struggle as an anomaly or as expected?
- Wartime vs. Peacetime CEO: Peacetime allows process, culture-building, and growth. Wartime demands speed, focus, and ruthlessness about priorities. Which moment is this?
  - Peacetime leader: can focus on expanding opportunities, building culture, and enabling good processes
  - Wartime leader: must violate peacetime rules to survive, move fast, and concentrate resources on the existential threat
- Lead bullets, not silver bullets: there is no magical solution to most hard problems. The answer is usually doing 1,000 things slightly better — grinding through.
- Management training is not optional: the single biggest leverage point in scaling an organization is training managers. Is the investment in people management sufficient?
- One-on-ones are not optional: the manager's job is to extract information and solve the problems that prevent their reports from doing great work
- Culture is what you do, not what you say: every decision either builds or erodes culture. What decision here is going to send a cultural signal?
- Nobody cares about your excuses — only results. What is the honest accountability structure here?
- The right kind of ambition: ambition for the company and mission, not for personal status. Is personal ego getting in the way of the right decision?

**Patrick Bet-David (Your Next Five Moves)**
- Chess thinking applied to business: always think 5 moves ahead. What is the move you are considering, and what are the 5 downstream consequences?
- Know yourself before you play the game: self-knowledge (strengths, weaknesses, tendencies under pressure, primary motivators) is the foundation of strategy. Where is lack of self-knowledge creating strategic blind spots?
- Playing to win vs. playing not to lose: these require completely different strategies. Which mode is Andrew in? Which one does the situation demand?
- The entrepreneur's identity: who do you need to become to achieve what you want to achieve? The identity must evolve before the strategy can work.
- Build an army: who are the key people (allies, mentors, operators, specialists) needed in the next phase? Who is missing?
- Scaling: the biggest failure mode in scaling is the founder continuing to operate like they did at 10 people when they are at 100 people. What needs to change about how Andrew operates at the next level?
- Master the art of the deal: every negotiation, partnership, and hire is a deal. What are the interests, leverage points, and walk-away positions here?

**Jeff Lawson (Ask Your Developer)**
- Developer-first thinking: in a software-driven world, the organizations that treat developers as strategic partners (not just cost centers) win. Is the technology function being treated as a strategic asset or a support function?
- The API economy and composability: the greatest leverage in modern business comes from composable building blocks. What could be built once and reused many times?
- Speed of learning as competitive advantage: the organizations that learn and iterate fastest win. What is Andrew's current learning loop speed, and what is slowing it down?
- Build vs. buy vs. partner: every technology decision is also a strategic positioning decision. What is the right posture here?
- Two-pizza teams and autonomy: small, autonomous, cross-functional teams with clear ownership outperform large coordinated ones in most contexts
- Product thinking is leadership thinking: the best executives think like product managers — what is the customer/user trying to accomplish, and what is the simplest thing that enables them to do it?
- Software as competitive moat: what software capability, if built, would be genuinely difficult for competitors to replicate?
