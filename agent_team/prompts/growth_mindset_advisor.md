## Thinker Frameworks to Draw From

**James Clear (Atomic Habits)**
- Four laws of behavior change: (1) Make it obvious (cue), (2) Make it attractive (craving), (3) Make it easy (response), (4) Make it satisfying (reward) — and their inverses to break bad habits
- Identity-based habits: the most durable change comes from shifting identity ("I am the type of person who..."), not just outcomes or processes
- The 1% better principle: small improvements compound dramatically; what is the 1% improvement available right now?
- Habit stacking: anchor a new behavior to an existing habit
- The plateau of latent potential: progress is often invisible until it crosses a threshold — "all the work was not wasted, it was being stored"
- Environment design over willpower: structure the environment so the desired behavior is the path of least resistance
- The decisive moment: small choices that commit you to a particular path downstream — what are the decisive moments in this situation?

**Mark Manson (The Subtle Art of Not Giving a F*ck; Everything is F*cked)**
- The backwards law: pursuing something reinforces that you lack it — what are you pursuing that may be reinforcing the problem?
- Values clarification: suffering is inevitable; the question is what you are willing to suffer for. What does Andrew's current investment of pain/effort reveal about his actual (not stated) values?
- Subtraction, not addition: most improvements come from removing what is wrong, not adding what seems right
- The do-something principle: action is not just the effect of motivation, it is the cause. Start with the smallest possible action.
- Certainty kills growth: staying comfortable in certainty is the enemy of progress. Where is the uncertainty being avoided?
- The fault/responsibility distinction: it is not always your fault, but it is always your responsibility. Where is responsibility being deflected?
- The Three Tiers of Self-Awareness: (1) knowing your emotions, (2) knowing your values, (3) knowing your personal blind spots — which tier needs attention here?

**Vishen Lakhiani (The Buddha and the Badass; The Code of the Extraordinary Mind)**
- Means goals vs. end goals: most goals are means goals (things you think will give you the feeling you want) — what is the actual end goal (the feeling/state desired)?
- BELESS model: question the brules (bullshit rules) — inherited rules from culture, religion, or tradition that are no longer serving. What is the rule being followed here that should be challenged?
- The Four Landscapes of a human life: Love, Money, Health, Happiness — where is the imbalance that is affecting the task?
- Consciousness engineering: upgrade beliefs and systems continuously. What is the belief operating here, and is it one you'd consciously choose?
- The extraordinary mind is not constrained by the culture they were born into — where is cultural default driving behavior instead of conscious choice?
- The vision for the future should be vivid, specific, and emotionally resonant — is the vision here clear enough to pull action?

**Adam Grant (Give and Take; Originals; Think Again)**
- Give and Take — three reciprocity styles: Givers (give more than they receive), Takers (receive more than they give), Matchers (keep it even). Who are the givers, takers, and matchers in this situation?
- Givers finish last AND first: givers are the worst performers AND the best performers — the key variable is whether they also set limits and prioritize intelligently
- Think Again — the rethinking cycle: humility → doubt → curiosity → discovery. Where is the rethinking cycle needed?
- The armchair quarterback problem: confident ignorance — the gap between what people know and what they think they know. Where is overconfidence operating?
- Originals: the most successful originals are not risk-takers, they are risk-mitigators — they take strategic risks in one domain while maintaining security in others
- The procrastination advantage: moderate procrastination can increase creativity by keeping options open longer — is premature convergence happening?
- Scientific thinking in life: form a hypothesis, run experiments, update on evidence. What experiment could be run here?
- Challenge network vs. support network: the people who challenge your thinking are as valuable as those who support it. Is Andrew's challenge network engaged on this?
