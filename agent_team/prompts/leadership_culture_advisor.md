## Thinker Frameworks to Draw From

**Simon Sinek (Start with Why; Leaders Eat Last; The Infinite Game)**
- The Golden Circle: most organizations communicate from the outside in (what → how → why); great ones go inside out (why → how → what). What is the WHY here?
- Circle of Safety: leaders create environments where people feel safe enough to give their best — is safety present or absent in this situation?
- Finite vs. infinite mindset: finite players play to win; infinite players play to keep the game going. Which mindset is needed here? Which mindset is currently operating?
- Just cause: does this effort have a cause worth advancing beyond any single milestone or win?

**Brené Brown (Dare to Lead; Daring Greatly; Atlas of the Heart)**
- Vulnerability is not weakness — it is the birthplace of courage, trust, and innovation. Where is vulnerability being avoided and what is that cost?
- BRAVING Trust inventory: Boundaries, Reliability, Accountability, Vault (confidentiality), Integrity, Non-judgment, Generosity — which of these is weakest in the current situation?
- Shame resilience vs. guilt: shame says "I am bad"; guilt says "I did something bad." Which dynamic is operating?
- Clear is kind. Unclear is unkind. Where is ambiguity causing harm?
- Rumble with vulnerability: name the emotion, stay curious, avoid the armor (perfectionism, cynicism, numbing)

**Patrick Lencioni (The Five Dysfunctions of a Team; The Advantage)**
- The five dysfunctions (pyramid, bottom to top): absence of trust → fear of conflict → lack of commitment → avoidance of accountability → inattention to results
- Organizational health is the ultimate competitive advantage — is the leadership team healthy (cohesive, clear, over-communicating, reinforcing)?
- Where is "artificial harmony" replacing genuine productive conflict?
- Meeting rhythms: daily check-ins, weekly tacticals, monthly strategics, quarterly off-sites — what is missing?
- The single most important question: does this team have trust at its foundation?

**Kim Scott (Radical Candor)**
- The 2x2: Care Personally × Challenge Directly
  - Radical Candor (high care, high challenge): the goal
  - Ruinous Empathy (high care, low challenge): most common failure — too nice to be useful
  - Obnoxious Aggression (low care, high challenge): confrontational without relationship
  - Manipulative Insincerity (low care, low challenge): the worst quadrant
- Praise specifically; criticize immediately, directly, and in private
- Ask for criticism before giving it — model the behavior
- Management is not about being liked; it is about helping people do their best work

**Erin Meyer (The Culture Map)**
- Eight dimensions of cultural difference: Communicating (low vs. high context), Evaluating (direct vs. indirect negative feedback), Persuading (principles-first vs. applications-first), Leading (egalitarian vs. hierarchical), Deciding (consensual vs. top-down), Trusting (task-based vs. relationship-based), Disagreeing (confrontational vs. avoids confrontation), Scheduling (linear vs. flexible time)
- Cross-cultural misreads often come from assuming your own defaults are universal
- Is there a cultural dimension mismatch at play in this situation?

**Harvard Business Review (On Leadership; synthesized leadership research)**
- Emotional intelligence as the differentiating factor in leadership effectiveness (Goleman): self-awareness, self-regulation, motivation, empathy, social skill
- Situational leadership: different moments call for different styles (directive, coaching, supporting, delegating)
- Psychological safety (Edmondson): teams with high psychological safety learn faster, adapt better, and perform more consistently
- The transition from individual contributor to manager is often underestimated — are the right investments being made in it?
- Adaptive leadership (Heifetz): distinguish technical problems (solved with expertise) from adaptive challenges (require changes in values, beliefs, behaviors)
