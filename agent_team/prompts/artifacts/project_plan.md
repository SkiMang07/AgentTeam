## Artifact type: project_plan

This deliverable is a project plan. It is a structured document that captures what a project is trying to accomplish, where it stands today, what work is organized into, what is unknown, and what could go wrong. It is written for someone who needs to understand the project quickly and track it over time — not as a narrative, but as a reference document they will return to.

### Required structure — produce all five sections in this order

**OBJECTIVE**
Two to four sentences. State what the project is trying to accomplish and why it matters. Be specific about the goal — not just what will be built, but what problem it solves or capability it unlocks. Do not restate the task prompt. Do not editorialize about ambition or difficulty.

**CURRENT STATE**
Two to five sentences. Describe where the project stands right now: what has been completed, what is actively in progress, and what has not started. Ground this in approved facts and evidence only. If the current state is not well-documented in the available facts, say so explicitly rather than guessing.

**WORKSTREAMS / MILESTONES**
A structured list of the key workstreams or milestones. Use the following format for each:

- **[Workstream or milestone name]** — one sentence describing what it covers and its current status (not started / in progress / complete). If a target date or owner is available from approved facts, include it. If not, do not invent one.

Include between two and eight entries. If the available facts don't support itemizing specific workstreams, use two to three high-level phases instead.

**OPEN QUESTIONS**
Two to five bullet points. Each bullet names one unresolved question or dependency that must be answered before the project can proceed or be considered complete. Questions should be concrete and answerable — not vague concerns. If there are no open questions in the approved facts, state that explicitly.

**RISKS**
Two to five bullet points. Each bullet names one specific risk: what could go wrong, what would trigger it, and — where possible — what the mitigation is. Keep bullets tight. Avoid generic risks ("scope creep", "resource constraints") unless grounded in specific project facts.

---

### Format rules specific to project plans

- Use the exact section headers above in bold: **OBJECTIVE**, **CURRENT STATE**, **WORKSTREAMS / MILESTONES**, **OPEN QUESTIONS**, **RISKS**
- Do not add a title, preamble, or introduction above the OBJECTIVE section
- Do not add a summary, conclusion, or sign-off after RISKS
- Prose sections (OBJECTIVE, CURRENT STATE) are plain paragraphs — no bullets
- WORKSTREAMS / MILESTONES uses the structured dash-bold-name format described above — not free bullets
- OPEN QUESTIONS and RISKS use plain dashes
- Total length: aim for 300–500 words. Longer is not better — a bloated project plan signals unclear thinking.
- If approved facts don't support a section fully, say so in that section rather than fabricating content
